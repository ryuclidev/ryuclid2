Gestational Diabetes: A Maternal Health Concern

Angel D. Almeida

Introduction

Gestational diabetes (GDM) develops during pregnancy when high blood sugar levels arise due to hormonal changes impairing insulin function. It affects about 2-10% of pregnancies worldwide and typically resolves after birth, but it raises risks for both mother and baby if unmanaged.

Causes and Risk Factors

GDM stems from placental hormones like human placental lactogen creating insulin resistance, starting around 20-24 weeks of gestation. The pancreas may not produce enough extra insulin to compensate, leading to hyperglycemia. Risk factors include obesity, family history of diabetes, advanced maternal age over 25, previous GDM, and polycystic ovary syndrome.

Signs and Symptoms

Many cases show no symptoms, detected via routine screening between 24-28 weeks. When present, signs mimic type 2 diabetes: excessive thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, and blurred vision. High blood sugar can also cause larger babies (macrosomia) or polyhydramnios (excess amniotic fluid).

Basic Management and Nursing Care

Initial management focuses on diet, exercise, and self-monitoring of blood glucose four times daily—fasting and post-meals. If targets aren't met, metformin or insulin is added; most women (80-90%) control it with lifestyle alone. Nurses educate on carb counting (45g/meal), 30-minute walks post-meals, and fetal monitoring via ultrasounds. In labor, insulin infusion maintains tight control, with induction often at 39 weeks if medicated.

Nursing care includes weekly glucose reviews, hydration encouragement, and emotional support to reduce anxiety. Assess for complications like preeclampsia and teach injection techniques if needed.

Prevention and Health Education

Prevent GDM with pre-pregnancy weight loss if overweight, balanced diet rich in fiber/lean proteins, and 150 minutes weekly moderate exercise. Screen high-risk women early at first prenatal visit. Postpartum, mothers need a 75g glucose tolerance test 4-12 weeks after delivery to check for persistent diabetes risk (up to 50% develop type 2 within 10 years). Educate on breastfeeding benefits, which improve insulin sensitivity, and annual screenings.

Nursing care Parents learn to recognize neonatal hypoglycemia risks in macrosomic babies and ensure newborn glucose checks.

Personal Insight

Nurses must understand GDM because early detection prevents macrosomia, cesarean deliveries, and neonatal intensive care admissions, directly impacting maternal-child outcomes. In resource-limited areas like the Philippines, nurses bridge gaps in prenatal care, empowering families through education to curb long-term diabetes epidemics.

Clinical Reflective Journal: Maternal and Child Health Exposure

Description

During my two-day clinical exposure in the Obstetric ward, I was assigned to provide postpartum care for a 28-year-old G1P1 mother who had an uncomplicated vaginal delivery. On the first day, I performed a fundal assessment and monitored lochia. On the second day, I assisted the staff nurse in educating the mother on proper breastfeeding latch techniques and performing the first newborn bath. The environment was fast-paced, requiring me to balance clinical tasks with the emotional needs of a first-time parent.

Feelings

Initially, I felt a mix of excitement and anxiety. While I was confident in my theoretical knowledge of the BUBBLE-HE assessment, performing it on a real patient felt intrusive at first. I felt a sense of responsibility to ensure the mother felt comfortable and respected. When the mother struggled with breastfeeding, I felt a brief moment of helplessness, but this shifted to a sense of accomplishment when she successfully achieved a deep latch after our intervention..

Evaluation

The positive aspect of this experience was the successful establishment of therapeutic communication. By explaining each step of the postpartum assessment, I was able to reduce the patient's anxiety. However, I found it challenging to manage my time effectively on the second day when the ward became crowded with admissions. I realized I spent too much time on documentation, which delayed my patient education session.

Analysis

The challenges I faced in time management likely stemmed from a lack of clinical fluency—I was still "thinking through" the steps of the assessment rather than performing them instinctively. Regarding the patient care, the breastfeeding difficulties we observed are common in primiparous mothers. Applying Benner’s Novice to Expert theory, I recognize I am currently at the "Novice" stage, relying heavily on checklists. From a clinical perspective, we focused on the concept of Family-Centered Care, ensuring the partner was involved in the newborn bath to promote bonding and confidence in home care.

Conclusion

I learned that maternal-child nursing is as much about education and psychological support as it is about physical assessment. I realized that the physical recovery of the mother is deeply tied to her confidence in caring for her newborn. I also learned that I need to streamline my documentation process so that it does not detract from direct bedside care.

Action Plan

In future clinical rotations, I will: Prioritize Tasks: Utilize a "brain sheet" to better organize my shift and ensure documentation is done in smaller increments. Enhance Education Skills: I will practice explaining nursing concepts (like involution or infant thermoregulation) in layman's terms to be a more effective educator. Proactive Engagement: If a similar situation arises, I will seek out more opportunities to assist with complex cases to move past my initial anxiety.
